\end{column}
\begin{column}{0.52\textwidth}
  \small\textbf{Matthiessen (2015): registerial cartography}\\[0.3em]
  \footnotesize
  Register = functional variety of language\\
  according to context of use.\\[0.4em]
  \textbf{Field of activity} = what's going on:\\[0.2em]
  \begin{tabular}{@{}ll@{}}
    \textit{doing} & joint social activity \\
    \textit{enabling} & action modelled via instruction \\
    \textit{exploring} & public values debated \\
    \textit{reporting} & events chronicled \\
  \end{tabular}
  \vspace{0.4em}

  \textbf{Applied to DCD corpus:}\\[0.2em]
  \footnotesize
  Non-contestation: \textit{reporting} + \textit{doing}\\
  Contestation: \textit{enabling} + \textit{exploring}\\[0.3em]
  \hl{Verb register shift = governance gap\\
  made linguistically visible}\\[0.3em]
  \textcolor{dcgray}{\scriptsize Behavioral +21.5\%,
  enabling +13.4\% in contestation corpus}
\end{column}
\end{columns}
\note{The Matthiessen wheel maps the eight fields of activity. DCD non-contestation coverage sits in reporting and doing. When contestation events peak, the register shifts to enabling and exploring — regulatory action and public debate. This is the governance gap visible in the verb inventory.}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}[noframenumbering]{\faIcon{question-circle}\enspace Q: Why CITC and not CSS more broadly?}
\footnotesize
CITC's lexical-framing requirement is what makes the output theoretical, not just descriptive.\\[0.5em]
That's the discipline that protects against the most common reviewer objection:\\[0.3em]
\begin{quote}
  ``This is interesting, but what's the theoretical contribution?''
\end{quote}
\vspace{0.3em}
CITC explicitly distinguishes patterns from contributions. The test for RP.
\end{frame}
