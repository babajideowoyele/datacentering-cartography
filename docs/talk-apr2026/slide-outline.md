# Talk Outline: "Towards a Cartography of Datacentering: A Multimodal Research Agenda"

**Event:** Research Policy 11th Online ECR Conference
**Date:** Monday 27 April 2026, 2pm BST
**Format:** 10 minutes presentation + 2 minutes Q&A (Zoom)
**Audience:** Innovation scholars, Research Policy editors

---

## Talk Arc

The talk moves in three beats. It opens with a provocation: the infrastructure running the digital economy is a near-total blind spot in innovation studies, and bibliometric evidence makes this impossible to dismiss. It then introduces the theoretical apparatus — "datacentering" as a concept, and data centers as Complex Adaptive Systems — and shows what a properly multimodal research agenda looks like across four work packages. It closes with an open invitation: the project is building shared infrastructure, and collaboration is the point.

The strategic choice is to lead with the empirical gap (Slides 2–3) before introducing theory (Slides 4–5), so the framework arrives as a solution to a problem the audience has already felt.

### Slide 2 — The Empirical Provocation
**Time: ~1:30**

- 4,757 papers with "data center" in the title on OpenAlex (2010–2025)
- Growth rate: **372%** over 15 years — a field in rapid expansion
- Innovation studies' engagement: **zero papers** invoking MLP, TIS, SNM, or sociotechnical regimes
- Three disconnected silos: technical efficiency (~279 papers), environmental impact (~114), socio-political (~15)
- A rich ethnographic literature exists — Vonderau, Holt & Vonderau, Blum — but is entirely invisible to this audience

**Speaker notes:**
This is the core empirical finding. Nearly five thousand papers on data centers — and not one engages the theoretical frameworks that are standard equipment in this room. The field is growing fast and fragmenting into three silos that do not talk to each other. Innovation scholars are not reading the ethnographers, and the ethnographers are not publishing in RP. The infrastructure transition we are all living through is happening without us.

### Slide 3 — What "The Cloud" Conceals
**Time: ~1:00**

- "Cloud" is a political metaphor: it naturalises infrastructure, makes it seem weightless and placeless
- Reality: data centers are the fastest-growing category of electricity consumer in OECD countries
- They are physical: server halls, cooling towers, aquifers, diesel generators, land, fibre
- They are contested: planning disputes, water rights, noise ordinances, community campaigns
- They are governed — or not: from REIT ownership structures to municipal moratoriums

**Speaker notes:**
The word "cloud" is doing ideological work. It conceals the material substrate — the water, the land, the power. When we make the infrastructure visible, we immediately find the features innovation scholars are trained to analyse: actors, contestation, governance, regime change. The problem is not that there is nothing to study. The problem is the framing. My contribution is to offer a better one.

### Slide 4 — Introducing "Datacentering"
**Time: ~1:00**

- **Datacentering** = the active socio-material process through which digital infrastructure is continuously assembled, contested, and reproduced
- A gerund, not a noun: it foregrounds process over static object
- Draws on Orlikowski (2007): technology and social practice are constitutively entangled, not separately analysable
- Draws on Jasanoff & Kim (2015): imaginaries — "the cloud" — co-produce the material facts they appear merely to describe
- The concept names what the cloud metaphor suppresses

**Speaker notes:**
I am proposing "datacentering" as a theoretical concept, not just a description. The shift from noun to gerund matters: we are not studying data centers as fixed objects but the ongoing process of building, contesting, and reproducing them. Orlikowski gives us the sociomaterial entanglement; Jasanoff and Kim give us the imaginary critique. Together they let us ask: who is doing the datacentering, for whom, and what is being assembled or foreclosed in the process.

### Slide 5 — Theoretical Framework: CAS + Three Dimensions
**Time: ~1:00**

- Data centers treated as **Complex Adaptive Systems** (Phillips & Ritala 2019): heterogeneous actors, nonlinear dynamics, emergent outcomes
- Nonhuman actors are genuine actors: servers, cooling towers, aquifers, power grids — not backdrop
- Three analytical dimensions (after Phillips & Ritala):
  1. **Conceptual** — boundaries, actor constellations, imaginaries
  2. **Structural** — hierarchy, ownership, relationships (human + nonhuman)
  3. **Temporal** — dynamics, co-evolution, adaptation, emergence
- Geels (2002) MLP provides the multi-level scaffolding missing from existing data center literature

**Speaker notes:**
The Phillips and Ritala CAS framework gives us a disciplined way to study systems where human and nonhuman actors co-constitute outcomes — which is exactly what data centers are. The three dimensions map cleanly onto the four work packages I am about to describe. And MLP gives us the vocabulary of niches, regimes, and landscapes that the technical efficiency literature completely lacks. I am not importing theory for its own sake — I am using it to solve the silo problem I showed you on Slide 2.

### Slide 6 — WP1 + WP2: Conceptual Dimension
**Time: ~1:00**

- **WP1 — Territorial Anchors:** Corporate websites + city Wikipedia pages; mapping stated and implied geography; what actors claim about their spatial presence
- **WP2 — Strategic Invisibility:** Satellite imagery, corporate photography, news images; how data centers are visually represented (or absent) in public discourse
- Combined question: *How do actors construct the imaginaries that make datacentering legible or illegible?*
- Methods: web scraping, NLP topic modelling, computer vision classification
- Key concept: "strategic invisibility" — the deliberate management of material presence

**Speaker notes:**
The first two work packages address the conceptual dimension: how data centers are imagined, named, and represented. WP1 uses the public text that organisations produce about themselves — websites and Wikipedia — as data on territorial claims. WP2 treats visual data as evidence: who shows what, when, and what is systematically absent. The finding that data centers are architecturally designed to look like nothing — no windows, no signs, no human scale — is itself a sociotechnical fact that needs explaining.

---

### Slide 7 — WP3 + WP4: Structural and Temporal Dimensions
**Time: ~1:00**

- **WP3 — Ownership Cartography:** Crunchbase, REIT filings, investment databases; mapping the financial architecture of digital infrastructure
- **WP4 — Contestation Signals:** News archives + social media; detecting when communities mobilise against data center development, and what triggers response
- WP3 addresses the structural dimension: who owns the infrastructure, in what legal form, and with what governance consequences
- WP4 addresses the temporal dimension: how contestation unfolds over time, and what determines regime response
- Together they reconstruct the signal-to-response chain missing from existing accounts

**Speaker notes:**
WP3 makes visible something the technical literature never asks: who actually owns this infrastructure, and in what form? REIT structures allow data center ownership to be distributed across pension funds and retail investors in ways that completely change the political economy of contestation. WP4 then asks the temporal question: when communities push back, what determines whether regimes respond? The news archive and social media data give us a longitudinal signal we can analyse systematically.

---

### Slide 8 — Comparative Illustration: Virginia vs. Amsterdam
**Time: ~1:30**

- **Northern Virginia (Loudoun County):** world's highest concentration of data center capacity; REIT ownership dominant; active community campaigns; **no moratorium**
- **Amsterdam:** moratorium declared 2019, lifted 2022; full transition episode on record; documented by Vonderau (2019)
- Same contestation intensity in both places
- Governance response in Amsterdam — not in Virginia
- Preliminary interpretation: **regime structure determines the signal-to-response lag**
- This is exactly the kind of question MLP was designed to answer — and existing literature cannot

**Speaker notes:**
This is an illustrative comparison, not a finished finding — but it shows the analytical purchase of the framework. The same level of community contestation produces a moratorium in Amsterdam and nothing in Virginia. The variable that differs is not the intensity of the signal but the structure of the regime receiving it: planning law, ownership dispersal, municipal authority, the presence or absence of a coherent governance actor. MLP gives us the language for this. The existing data center literature does not.

### Slide 9 — The Multimodal Research Agenda
**Time: ~0:30**

- Four WPs = four data modalities: text, image, financial, temporal
- Each WP maps onto one or more of the three CAS dimensions
- Methods are open-source and reproducible: NLP, computer vision, network analysis, scraping pipelines
- Output: open facility index + shared code repository + open data — not just papers
- Timeline: WP1–WP2 piloted; WP3–WP4 in development

**Speaker notes:**
The agenda is multimodal by design, not by eclecticism. Each work package addresses a dimension of the CAS that the others cannot reach. The commitment to open methods and open data is not a nice-to-have — it is the only way this cartography gets built at the scale and resolution the problem requires.

If you work on energy transitions, regional innovation systems, or platform governance, there is a work package here that connects to your questions. ## Q&A Preparation Notes (2 minutes)

Anticipated questions and suggested responses:

- **"Is 'datacentering' just another name for infrastructure studies?"** — No. The gerund form is doing specific theoretical work: it foregrounds the active, contested, ongoing assembly rather than treating infrastructure as a stable substrate. The CAS framing also allows nonhuman actors in ways that most infrastructure studies do not.

- **"Why not just use Political Ecology or STS?"** — STS is foundational here (Orlikowski, Jasanoff). Political ecology is complementary. The contribution is bringing these into dialogue with MLP/TIS, which is where the innovation studies audience already sits.

- **"How do you handle the fact that data center firms are notoriously secretive?"** — That secrecy is itself a finding, operationalised in WP2 as "strategic invisibility." The multimodal approach is specifically designed to triangulate around information gaps: if the firm won't publish, the satellite image, the planning application, and the REIT filing still exist.

- **"What is the unit of analysis?"** — The datacentering process in a territory, over time — not the individual facility. The facility is the observation; the process is the unit.
