# WP4: Contestation Signals — The Temporal Layer of the CAS Assemblage

The first three work packages produce largely synchronic portraits of the datacentering assemblage: how operators narrate themselves, how facilities are rendered visible or invisible, how ownership is constituted through financialisation. WP4 introduces time. Its purpose is to detect, track, and compare the temporal arc of community contestation against data center development — from the first signs of organised opposition through to whatever regime response, if any, that opposition eventually produces.

The empirical core is a multi-source signal corpus built from three data streams. News archives (regional and national press via GDELT, NewsAPI, or MediaCloud) provide a longitudinal record of how contestation enters public discourse. Planning records — council minutes, formal objection filings, moratorium orders — supply the institutional trace of how public signal is translated into regulatory action. Social media data (X/Twitter, Reddit community boards) captures faster-moving, lower-threshold mobilisation that precedes or accompanies formal planning processes. Together these sources construct a *contestation signal*: a time-indexed, multi-actor record of resistance intensity and form.

Analytically, topic modelling (BERTopic applied to the news and social media corpus) surfaces the thematic clusters through which contestation is articulated — energy demand, noise, water consumption, land use conflict — and tracks how these frames shift over time. Event detection identifies discrete inflection points: first substantive press coverage, first formal planning objection, moratorium filing, and eventual regulatory decision. Longitudinal signal tracking then computes the *signal-to-response lag* — the elapsed time between measurable community mobilisation and any identifiable regime action — and compares trajectories across jurisdictions.

This lag variable is WP4's core theoretical contribution to the CAS argument. A CAS is characterised not only by its compositional structure but by the dynamics of adaptation: how components respond to disturbance signals generated by others. Contestation is precisely such a disturbance, and signal-to-response lag operationalises the key empirical question: under what structural conditions does community feedback produce adaptive regime response? When read alongside WP3's ownership structures — which determine how regulatory accountability is distributed across governance tiers — lag becomes a structural diagnostic: it reveals whether a given assemblage configuration is capable of translating local resistance into systemic adjustment.

The comparative design makes this concrete. Amsterdam declared a moratorium on new data center construction in 2019, halted development across the metropolitan region, and lifted the moratorium in 2022 under a differentiated framework requiring stringent energy-efficiency and water-use criteria. Anique Vonderau's (2019) ethnographic account provides the qualitative baseline; WP4 reconstructs the archival record of how that transition unfolded in news and planning documents. Northern Virginia — and Loudoun County specifically — presents the structural contrast: comparable contestation intensity across the same period, no moratorium, and an operator-friendly state permitting environment that dispersed local accountability rather than consolidating it. The variable that differs is not signal strength but regime structure. WP4 makes this contrast measurable.

What WP4 contributes that no other work package can is the dynamic, temporal dimension of the assemblage. WPs 1–3 reconstruct structure at a point in time; WP4 observes the system in motion — the moment at which community actors introduce disturbance and the assemblage either adapts or absorbs without change. This is also where the CAS framing becomes most directly relevant to policy: understanding what structural conditions shorten the signal-to-response lag is the practical upshot of treating datacentering as a complex adaptive system rather than a fixed infrastructural object.

At this stage, WP4 requires substantial construction. No news archive corpus has been assembled, no planning records collected, and the modelling pipelines do not yet exist. The operationalisation of key constructs — which events mark signal onset, which count as regime response, how to handle gradual regulatory transitions — requires conceptual work before computation can begin. The comparative design and theoretical motivation are clear; the infrastructure is not yet built.
