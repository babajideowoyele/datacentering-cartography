# Argument Structure: Towards a Cartography of Datacentering

**Working title:** Towards a Cartography of Datacentering: A Multimodal Research Agenda

**Target venue:** Research Policy (or comparable innovation studies journal)

**Drafted:** April 2026

---

## Draft Abstract

The physical infrastructure of the digital economy — data centers consuming an estimated 400 TWh of electricity annually, drawing on aquifers, land, and power grids across every inhabited continent — is a near-total blind spot in innovation studies. A bibliometric audit of 4,757 publications (OpenAlex, 2010–2025) finds zero engagement with the Multi-Level Perspective, Technological Innovation Systems, Strategic Niche Management, or sociotechnical regimes, and three non-communicating silos that treat the phenomenon as a technical optimisation problem rather than a socio-material one. This paper argues that the concept of *datacentering* — the active, contested, ongoing process through which digital infrastructure is assembled, reproduced, and contested — names what the dominant "cloud" metaphor suppresses: that the infrastructure is material, territorial, and governed, and that its dynamics exhibit the properties of Complex Adaptive Systems (CAS). Drawing on the CAS sensemaking framework of Phillips and Ritala (2019), we develop a four-part multimodal methodology that maps datacentering across conceptual, structural, and temporal dimensions through corporate text, satellite and visual imagery, financial ownership data, and contestation signals. An illustrative comparative case — Northern Virginia versus Amsterdam — demonstrates the framework's analytical purchase: the same intensity of community contestation produces a moratorium in Amsterdam and none in Virginia, and the variable that differs is not signal strength but regime structure. The paper concludes by arguing that the methodological response to a CAS-like phenomenon must itself be open and distributed, and proposes a community-maintained research commons as a theoretically motivated design choice, not an afterthought.

---

## Core Contribution

This paper makes a single, integrated contribution at the intersection of innovation theory, science and technology studies, and research methodology. It establishes that data center infrastructure constitutes a legitimate and long-neglected object for innovation scholarship; proposes *datacentering* as a theoretically grounded concept that reframes the object from a fixed asset into an active socio-material process; and develops a multimodal cartographic methodology — combining text, image, financial, and temporal data streams — that is adequate to the CAS-like complexity of that process. The paper's evidential anchor is a bibliometric demonstration that the gap is structural, not incidental; its theoretical anchor is the argument that the "cloud" metaphor actively produces the gap by making the phenomenon illegible to the frameworks that would otherwise engage it; and its methodological anchor is the demonstration, through the Virginia/Amsterdam comparison, that a CAS-informed multimodal approach generates explanations — of differential governance response, of dispersed accountability, of emergent moratorium — that existing single-method accounts cannot produce.

## Section Outline

---

### 1. Introduction

**What it does:** Opens with the empirical provocation — the bibliometric audit — and establishes the gap as a structural feature of the field rather than an oversight to be corrected by a single paper. It introduces the central puzzle: why does an infrastructure system that now accounts for a significant and rapidly growing share of OECD electricity consumption, that is actively contested in planning systems across multiple jurisdictions, and that exhibits all the features innovation scholars are trained to analyse — actors, governance, contestation, transition pressure — remain outside the field's theoretical gaze?

**Key move:** The opening argument is not normative ("we should study this") but diagnostic: the gap is an effect of a specific metaphor. "The cloud" performs ideological work by naturalising and de-materialising the infrastructure, and as long as innovation studies accepts the metaphor, the phenomenon remains invisible. The introduction closes by stating that the paper offers both a conceptual remedy (the shift from "cloud" to "datacentering") and a methodological one (multimodal cartography), and flags the comparative case and the open exchange framing as the paper's two main empirical and design contributions.

**Scope:** Approximately 1,000–1,200 words. The bibliometric gap is introduced here but detailed in a subsequent sub-section of the methodology. The theoretical apparatus is named but not developed.

---

### 2. Theoretical Framing

**What it does:** Develops the paper's two theoretical moves in sequence. First, it introduces *datacentering* as a concept — distinguishing the gerund form from the noun "data center" and grounding it in Orlikowski's (2007) sociomaterial entanglement and Jasanoff and Kim's (2015) sociotechnical imaginaries. Second, it introduces the CAS sensemaking framework (Phillips and Ritala 2019) — not as a formal analytical model to be operationalised, but as a lens that names the properties of the phenomenon that any adequate methodology must address: heterogeneous actor constellations, nonlinear dynamics, and emergent outcomes that cannot be read off from inputs.

**Key move:** The section argues that the two theoretical moves are mutually reinforcing, not merely additive. *Datacentering* identifies what is happening and who is doing it; the CAS framing specifies why it is hard to see and why single-method accounts systematically fail to capture it. The section also positions the paper's engagement with MLP (Geels 2002) and TIS: these frameworks are not applied directly, but they are the theoretical vocabulary that makes the Virginia/Amsterdam comparison tractable and that explains why the bibliometric gap constitutes a loss for the field, not just a gap in the literature.

**Scope:** Approximately 1,500–1,800 words. The section is conceptual, not a literature review, and should move briskly through theoretical positions it draws on rather than rehearsing them in full.

---

### 3. Methodology: A Multimodal Cartographic Approach

**What it does:** Presents the four-work-package methodology as a designed response to the CAS properties identified in the theoretical framing, and explains the mapping between data modalities and CAS dimensions. WP1 (corporate and territorial text) and WP2 (satellite and visual imagery) address the conceptual dimension — how actors construct imaginaries and manage visibility; WP3 (financial and ownership data) addresses the structural dimension — how ownership dispersal through REIT structures creates fragmented governance; WP4 (news and social media contestation signals) addresses the temporal dimension — when communities mobilise and what determines whether the regime responds. The section also presents the bibliometric audit methodology as evidence for the structural gap claim made in the introduction.

**Key move:** Multimodality here is not eclecticism. Each data modality is necessary because each CAS dimension is methodologically inaccessible to the others: corporate text cannot reveal ownership structure; satellite imagery cannot capture the temporal dynamics of contestation; financial filings cannot show how imaginary construction shapes what becomes legible as a planning problem. The section argues that strategic opacity — the deliberate management of data center visibility — is itself a sociotechnical fact that the multimodal design is specifically constructed to triangulate around. A firm that will not publish can still be found in a REIT filing, a planning application, and a satellite image.

**Scope:** Approximately 1,800–2,200 words. Includes a summary table mapping WPs to CAS dimensions, data sources, and methods. Bibliometric findings presented with basic visualisation.

---

### 4. Illustrative Case: Virginia and Amsterdam

**What it does:** Presents the Northern Virginia (Loudoun County) versus Amsterdam comparison as a structured illustration of what the multimodal CAS framework makes visible that existing accounts cannot explain. Both sites exhibit comparable contestation intensity: active community campaigns, documented planning disputes, political attention. Amsterdam produced a moratorium from 2019 to 2022 — a full transition episode on the record, documented by Vonderau (2019). Northern Virginia, the world's single largest concentration of data center capacity, produced none. The case traces the structural variable that differs: Amsterdam's moratorium was made possible by a coherent municipal governance actor, a planning system with the legal authority to act, and an ownership structure that created accountable counterparties. Northern Virginia's REIT-dominated ownership model disperses accountability across pension funds and retail investors in ways that insulate the system from local political pressure.

**Key move:** The case is illustrative, not causal. Its function is to demonstrate the analytical purchase of the framework: the CAS sensemaking dimensions — conceptual (how each city imagined its relationship to the infrastructure), structural (who owned it and in what legal form), and temporal (how contestation unfolded and at what pace) — generate an explanation for the differential outcome that neither the technical efficiency literature nor the ethnographic single-site tradition can produce alone. The case also shows that the framework's temporal dimension is not merely descriptive: the Amsterdam moratorium and its lifting constitute a co-evolutionary episode with identifiable triggers and regime-level response, and that episode can be mapped with the WP4 methodology.

**Scope:** Approximately 1,500–1,800 words. The section is explicitly positioned as illustrative; it draws on published sources (Vonderau 2019; secondary reporting on Loudoun County planning history) rather than original empirical collection, and acknowledges this as a limitation to be addressed in subsequent work.

---

### 5. Discussion

**What it does:** Draws out three implications of the framework and the case that extend beyond the immediate empirical material. First, it argues that the "open exchange" design of the research infrastructure — the community-maintained facility index, shared pipelines, and open data — is not a methodological convenience but a theoretically motivated response to the CAS structure of the phenomenon: because datacentering is too large, too distributed, and too deliberately opaque for any single researcher or team to map, the research commons is the only methodologically adequate response. Second, it connects the paper's argument to the broader innovation studies agenda by specifying what a properly instrumented data center literature would contribute to MLP, TIS, and platform governance research. Third, it discusses the limits of the CAS framing — particularly the risk that "complexity" becomes a theoretical free pass — and argues that the three-dimensional sensemaking structure (Phillips and Ritala) disciplines the application in ways that prevent this.

**Key move:** The discussion reframes the open exchange argument from a nice-to-have into a theoretical necessity. A CAS-like phenomenon requires distributed observation; distributed observation requires shared infrastructure; shared infrastructure requires a research commons with explicit governance and quality standards. This is the move that connects the paper's methodology section to CONTRIBUTING.md: the contributing guide is not supplementary material, it is part of the methodological argument.

**Scope:** Approximately 1,200–1,500 words.

---

### 6. Conclusion

**What it does:** Returns to the bibliometric gap and reframes it as a solvable problem — not by a single paper but by a research programme. Restates the core contribution in compressed form: a concept (*datacentering*), a framework (CAS sensemaking applied to socio-material assemblages), a methodology (multimodal cartography), and a design principle (open exchange as theoretical necessity). Closes with a concrete statement of what the next empirical steps are — the systematic application of WP1–WP4 pipelines to the Northern Virginia and Amsterdam cases, and the extension to additional jurisdictions through the community-maintained facility index.

**Key move:** The conclusion should resist the temptation to overclaim. The paper's contribution is a research agenda, not a completed programme; the case is illustrative, not evidentiary; the methodology is designed, not yet fully deployed. The conclusion's function is to show that the agenda is tractable — that the gaps identified at the opening can be closed — and to make the invitation to collaborate explicit.

**Scope:** Approximately 600–800 words.

---

## How the Four Elements Connect

The paper's coherence depends on the argument that its four main elements are not parallel contributions but a single integrated one.

**The CAS framing** is necessary because it identifies *why* the phenomenon has been invisible to innovation studies: not because it lacks the right features, but because those features — heterogeneous actors, nonlinear dynamics, emergent outcomes — are only visible at a level of systemic analysis that single-method, single-site approaches cannot reach. CAS sensemaking is the analytical warrant for multimodality.

**The multimodal methodology** follows from the CAS framing: each dimension of the system (conceptual, structural, temporal) requires a different data modality, and the opacity of the phenomenon is not a data problem to be solved but a sociotechnical fact to be triangulated around. The four work packages are not four separate studies; they are four facets of a single cartographic project.

**The Virginia/Amsterdam case** demonstrates what the framework produces when applied: not a thicker description of a single site, but an explanation of differential outcomes across sites that turns on structural and temporal features that no existing account could name. This is the paper's proof of concept.

**The open exchange framing** closes the argument by showing that the methodological logic — distributed observation of a distributed phenomenon — cannot stop at the research team's boundary. The research commons is the methodological apparatus scaled to the object's actual complexity. CONTRIBUTING.md is not documentation; it is the governance architecture of a distributed observatory.

Together, the four elements form a single claim: that the cloud metaphor has concealed a socio-material phenomenon of major analytical significance, that the right framework for studying it is now available, that an adequate methodology requires multimodal and collaborative design, and that the preliminary evidence — bibliometric, comparative, conceptual — is already sufficient to justify the programme.
