# WP2: Visual and Strategic Invisibility

## Contribution to the Conceptual Dimension of Data Center Assemblages

Data centers are built, at considerable expense, to look like nothing. The absence of windows, the suppression of signage, the warehouse-scale massing that refuses human scale — these are not incidental features of industrial economy but deliberate acts of visual engineering. WP2 treats this designed invisibility as a sociotechnical fact in its own right, one that encodes relations of power, governance, and contested legitimacy into the built fabric. Its central claim is that what operators choose not to show is as analytically significant as what they do show, and that systematic analysis of the visual register — across satellite imagery, corporate photography, and the metadata that surrounds it — can surface the conceptual contours of the data center assemblage: its boundaries, its self-projections, and the imaginaries it works to sustain.

### Data Sources and Method

WP2 operates across two parallel streams of visual material. The first is Sentinel-2 satellite imagery acquired via the Google Earth Engine API (with Copernicus Data Space Ecosystem as a fallback), fetched at 10-metre resolution for a 500-metre bounding box around each facility coordinate. This stream produces an overhead, state-derived account of the physical footprint: the building envelopes, cooling infrastructure, car parks, and the spatial relationships to adjacent land uses that operators do not control and cannot redact. The second stream is corporate-published imagery scraped from operator websites — sustainability pages, facilities pages, about-us sections — collected with full respect for `robots.txt` and assembled into per-operator manifests that record not only the image files but the alt text and surrounding page context for each. Together, the two streams triangulate between what an operator chooses to project and what remote sensing independently records.

The analytical pipeline in `analyse_visual_content.py` processes the corporate corpus in two stages. First, Pillow-based metrics — dimensions, aspect ratio, dominant colour — are extracted for every image, establishing a basic visual vocabulary. Second, a content classification pass assigns each image to one of three heuristic categories: *abstract* (server rooms, racks, logos, sustainability iconography, diagrams), *exterior* (building facades, campuses, aerial views, cooling towers), or *unknown*. Classification draws on alt text keyword matching as a baseline and, optionally, on zero-shot CLIP classification or a fine-tuned ResNet50 for content-based labelling. From these per-image labels, a scalar *visual abstraction score* — the ratio of abstract to total labelled images — is computed per operator. This score operationalises the degree to which an operator's public visual self-presentation is decoupled from the material specificity of its facilities.

The comparison stage in `compare_jurisdictions.py` merges operator-level abstraction scores with a facility index field, `jurisdiction_disclosure_level`, and tests for systematic variation using a Kruskal-Wallis H-test — a non-parametric choice suited to bounded scores and small operator samples. The output is a summary table of mean abstraction scores by disclosure group and, where the test is feasible, an effect-size estimate.

### Strategic Invisibility as a Sociotechnical Fact

The conceptual contribution of WP2 lies in treating the visual regime not as an epiphenomenon of corporate communications strategy but as a constitutive element of the assemblage. Drawing on the Phillips and Ritala (2019) CAS framing, WP2 addresses the *conceptual dimension*: the boundaries operators draw around themselves, the perspectives they articulate, and the imaginaries they seek to naturalise. Strategic invisibility operates at multiple registers simultaneously. Architecturally, facilities are engineered to be visually indistinguishable from distribution warehouses, suppressing the spatial claim that a 50-megawatt load on a regional grid would otherwise make visible. Communicatively, operators substitute imagery of server racks, fibre optics, and green-leaf sustainability graphics for exterior location shots — a substitution that emphasises the universal and abstract while erasing the local and material. The *abstraction score* captures this substitution directly: a high score marks an operator whose visual self-presentation performs placelessness.

This is not merely a communications choice. It is a governance claim. Abstracted visual self-presentation supports the framing of data centers as cloud — infrastructure that is everywhere and nowhere — at precisely the moment when planning authorities, energy regulators, and communities are beginning to contest that framing. The visual register is one site at which the conceptual boundary of the assemblage is actively maintained against encroachment by the social.

### Comparative Axis: Loudoun County and Amsterdam

The USA–Europe comparison sharpens the argument. In Loudoun County, Northern Virginia — the most concentrated data center market on earth — the hyperscale campus model produces facilities that are individually enormous and collectively dominant in the landscape but which, beyond that sheer scale, maintain strict visual anonymity: unmarked buildings, suppressed signage, featureless facades. The satellite stream captures this massing objectively; the corporate stream shows the corresponding representational strategy, which tends toward interior abstraction and sustainability iconography. Disclosure requirements in this jurisdiction are characterised in the facility index as low. The predicted pattern is high abstraction scores, reflecting operators whose visual self-presentation is systematically decoupled from place.

The Amsterdam case presents a different configuration. Dutch planning law, the regional AMS-IX ecosystem, and the ArenA area's mixed residential-commercial character have all generated a higher-disclosure environment in which operators face greater pressure to account for their footprint — energy and water consumption especially, under the datacenter covenant and subsequent regulatory attention. The satellite imagery here captures facilities embedded in a denser urban fabric, and the expectation is that corporate imagery is more likely to include exterior shots that situate the facility in relation to its surroundings, producing lower abstraction scores. The Kruskal-Wallis test operationalises whether this cross-jurisdictional difference in visual regime is systematic rather than idiosyncratic.

The comparison thus maps the conceptual dimension across regimes: different disclosure environments produce not just different information flows but different visual grammars through which operators constitute themselves as actors with or without a place.

### Analytical Outputs

WP2 produces four primary outputs. The `data/processed/wp2_visual_summary.csv` file aggregates abstraction scores, image-type distributions, and basic visual metrics per operator and is the key quantitative artefact. The `outputs/figures/wp2_abstraction_by_jurisdiction.png` bar chart summarises the cross-jurisdictional comparison with uncertainty bounds. The satellite imagery archive in `data/raw/visual/satellite/` provides the remote-sensing baseline against which corporate visual strategies can be read. The manifests in `data/raw/visual/corporate/` preserve the raw evidence — images, alt text, context — for qualitative review.

The notebook `notebooks/wp2/01_explore_corporate_imagery.ipynb` supports interactive exploration: image-count inspection, alt text frequency analysis, sample visual review, and abstraction score distribution plotting. It also carries forward two explicit markers of what remains open: the need for manual annotation of a stratified sample to validate the heuristic taxonomy, and the possibility of temporal analysis if operator websites are scraped at multiple time points.

### What Is Built, What Remains

The pipeline is operationally complete: satellite imagery acquisition, corporate scraping, computer vision classification, and jurisdiction comparison all run end-to-end. The facility index currently carries three seed entries — an AWS campus in Northern Virginia, an Equinix facility in Amsterdam, and a Digital Realty site in Dublin — which is sufficient to validate the pipeline architecture but not to support the jurisdictional comparison statistically. The most pressing open task is expanding the facility index and re-running collection to reach a sample large enough for the Kruskal-Wallis test to be meaningful. The second open task is replacing the heuristic image classifier with a validated taxonomy: CLIP zero-shot classification is implemented and ready but requires a human-annotated sample against which to assess its performance. Until both are done, the abstraction score should be read as an indicator rather than a measurement. What is already established, and what WP2 contributes to the paper argument independently of sample scale, is the conceptual claim: that strategic invisibility is a legible, measurable property of the visual assemblage, and that it varies with disclosure regime in ways that map onto the broader governance dynamics the paper is trying to reconstruct.
