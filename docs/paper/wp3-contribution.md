## WP3: Ownership Cartography and the Financialisation of Digital Infrastructure

The third work package addresses the structural dimension of the CAS framework by asking a question that the technical data center literature almost never poses: who actually owns this infrastructure, in what legal form, and with what consequences for governance? WP3 maps the financial architecture underlying data center development, drawing on four primary data sources — the OpenCorporates company registry API, REIT annual filings and SEC disclosures, Crunchbase investment records, and supplementary investment databases — to trace ownership chains from individual facilities through to their ultimate beneficial owners.

The methodological anchor is `fetch_opencorporates.py`, a pipeline that resolves operator and owner company names from the project's facility index against the OpenCorporates registry. For each entity in the input, the script retrieves canonical legal identities, jurisdiction codes, incorporation dates, company type designations, and registered officer networks. This corporate-registry layer is a prerequisite for the ownership graph: before tracing who owns a facility, one must establish the formal legal identity of every node in the chain — a step that is non-trivial when operating company names diverge from registered names, when subsidiaries share partial names with their parents, or when holding structures pass through multiple jurisdictions. The script handles these complications through a normalisation and best-match heuristic that strips legal suffixes and compares names after reducing to a canonical form, falling back to the highest-ranked registry result where exact matching fails. Output for each company is written to a structured directory: raw search results, the full company record, an officer list where the API tier permits, and a per-company fetch log — producing an auditable trail from input name to registered identity.

What this pipeline reveals, when combined with REIT filings and investment data, is a structural condition that this paper terms *financialisation of digital infrastructure*: the progressive separation of facility operation from facility ownership, mediated by financial instruments that distribute ownership across large and largely anonymous pools of capital. In the United States, this pattern is most advanced. The dominant ownership form in Northern Virginia's data center market — the highest-concentration cluster in the world — is the Real Estate Investment Trust. Equinix, Digital Realty, Iron Mountain, and a set of non-traded private REITs collectively control the majority of leasable capacity in Loudoun County. The REIT structure is not merely a tax-efficiency mechanism; it is a governance-relevant form. By distributing beneficial ownership across retail investors, pension funds, and sovereign wealth funds, it dissolves the concentrated accountability that would otherwise attach to a single identifiable owner. No single shareholder holds a position large enough to bear reputational or political pressure from local contestation. The governance consequences are direct: when community campaigns, planning bodies, or municipal authorities seek a counterparty capable of making binding commitments about energy sourcing, water use, or capacity limits, the REIT structure provides no such counterparty. The operating entity — the tenant REIT or colocation operator — is legally distinct from the asset-owning entity, which is in turn only one fund within a diversified investment vehicle whose beneficiaries are spatially dispersed and collectively inert.

The European ownership landscape is structurally different in ways that matter for regime response. Amsterdam's data center market, which experienced a formal moratorium between 2019 and 2022 and remains the primary European comparator in this study, exhibits a more varied ownership ecology: operator-owned campuses (Equinix operates but also owns significant assets; AMS-IX is a member-owned cooperative exchange), municipal utility-adjacent development in some cases, and sovereign wealth fund investment that, unlike dispersed REIT ownership, is concentrated enough to be a visible and accountable actor. The Netherlands' planning law also structures accountability differently, creating a clearer channel from municipal authority to identifiable ownership entity. This is not to argue that European ownership structures are uniformly more accountable — the data-gathering is at an early stage and European markets are themselves becoming increasingly REIT-adjacent as US-listed REITs extend their European portfolios — but rather that the structural composition of ownership shapes the surface area available for governance intervention. Where ownership is concentrated and jurisdictionally legible, a moratorium is a negotiable instrument. Where ownership is dispersed across index funds and retail accounts in dozens of countries, the same instrument becomes almost unenforceable in practice.

The analytical outputs WP3 is designed to produce are two: an ownership network, in which nodes are legal entities (facilities, operating companies, holding companies, investment funds, ultimate beneficial owners) and edges are ownership, directorship, or investment relationships; and an actor typology, distinguishing at minimum between vertically integrated operator-owners, colocation operators leasing REIT-held assets, financial asset managers with no operational function, and public or quasi-public entities. These two outputs together constitute the structural map that WP3 contributes to the broader CAS cartography — a representation of the hierarchical and relational architecture of the system, in the terms Phillips and Ritala (2019) use to specify the structural dimension of complex adaptive systems analysis.

As of April 2026, WP3 is at an early stage. The OpenCorporates pipeline is built and tested; the config template awaiting population with the full facility index company list. REIT filing extraction and the network-graph construction step have not yet been implemented. The comparative analysis of Virginia versus Amsterdam ownership structures is currently based on secondary sources and desk research rather than primary data queried through the pipeline. The priority development tasks are: populating the company config with entities from WP1's facility index; developing a complementary REIT-parsing script that extracts asset schedules from SEC EDGAR filings; building the network assembly step that joins OpenCorporates records, officer data, and REIT ownership data into a unified graph; and implementing the actor typology classifier. Until these components exist, WP3's structural findings remain indicative rather than systematic. What is already clear from the architecture, however, is the analytical necessity of this work package: no other component of the methodology reaches the financial and legal substrate through which ownership accountability — or its absence — is constituted.
